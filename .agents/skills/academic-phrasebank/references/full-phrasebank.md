# Academic Phrasebank Skill

**Source**: Morley, J. (2021). *Academic Phrasebank*. University of Manchester. 126 pp.
**Full PDF**: `/Users/sebastianmarshall/Desktop/Research/Academic-Phrasebank-2021.pdf`

## When to Use
When writing or editing academic prose: chapter text, journal manuscripts, review sections, discussions, conclusions. Use these patterns to replace AI-sounding constructions with established academic language.

---

## 1. Reporting Results (Figures and Tables)

### Referring to figures/tables
- Table X shows / displays / presents / provides / compares ...
- As shown in Figure X, ...
- It can be seen from the data in Table X that ...
- As can be seen from the table, ...
- From the graph above we can see that ...

### Highlighting significant data
- What stands out in the table is ...
- Closer inspection of the table shows ...
- The most striking result to emerge from the data is that ...
- An inspection of the data in Table X reveals that ...

### Stating positive results
- X was found to be positively correlated with Y.
- There was a significant positive correlation between X and Y.
- A significant increase in X was found (p < 0.05).

### Stating negative results
- No significant differences were found between ...
- X appeared to be unaffected by Y.
- There was no evidence that X has an influence on ...
- No increase in X was detected.

### Highlighting surprising results
- This result is somewhat counterintuitive.
- The most striking result to emerge from the data is that ...
- The most surprising aspect of the data is ...

## 2. Discussing Findings

### Comparing: supporting previous findings
- These results are consistent with data obtained in ...
- These results are in line with those of previous studies.
- These results are in agreement with those obtained by ...
- These results corroborate the ideas of X, who suggested that ...
- These results support previous research into this area which links X and Y.
- These results mirror those of the previous studies that have examined ...

### Comparing: contradicting previous findings
- This study has been unable to demonstrate that ...
- This finding is contrary to previous studies which have suggested that ...
- In contrast to earlier findings, no evidence of X was detected.
- However, the findings of the current study do not support the previous research.
- These results differ from X's estimate of Y, but they are broadly consistent with ...

### Suggesting hypotheses
- These findings suggest that ...
- It is possible, therefore, that ...
- The present study raises the possibility that ...
- This observation may support the hypothesis that ...
- According to these data, we can infer that ...

### Noting implications
- An implication of this is the possibility that ...
- One of the issues that emerges from these findings is ...
- This combination of findings provides some support for the premise that ...
- These findings suggest that / provide support for / challenge the notion that ...
- These findings have important implications for ...
- These findings raise intriguing questions regarding the nature and extent of ...

## 3. Being Cautious (Hedging)

### Distancing from a proposition
- It has been reported that ...
- According to recent reports, ...
- There is some evidence to suggest that ...
- Recent research has suggested that ...

### Cautious explanations
- These results may be due to ...
- It is likely / possible / probable that ...
- A likely explanation is that ...
- A possible explanation for this might be that ...
- This discrepancy could be attributed to ...
- The observed increase in X could be attributed to ...
- There are several possible explanations for this result.

### Cautious interpretation
- These data must be interpreted with caution because ...
- These results therefore need to be interpreted with caution.
- These findings cannot be extrapolated to all ...
- We cannot exclude the possibility that ...

### Cautious implications
- The findings of this study suggest that ...
- Taken together, these results suggest that ...
- The evidence from this study suggests that ...
- The data reported here appear to support the assumption that ...
- These initial results are suggestive of a link between X and Y.

### Avoiding over-generalisation
- In general, this requires ...
- X tends to ... / There is a tendency for X to ...
- X is generally assumed to play a role in ...

## 4. Explaining Causality

### Verbs indicating causality
- X may cause / can lead to / can result in / can give rise to Y.
- X is driven by / stems from / is caused by / can be attributed to Y.

### Nouns indicating causality
- One reason why X declined is that ...
- A consequence of X is Y.
- The most likely causes of X are ...

### Prepositional phrases
- ... owing to / because of / as a result of / as a consequence of ...

### Sentence connectors
- Therefore, / Consequently, / Because of this, / As a result (of this), ...

### Contributing agency
- X is a key factor in / a major influence on / an important determinant of Y.
- X has a significant impact on Y.
- X plays an important role in Y.
- Several factors are known to affect / influence / determine X.

### Contributing verbs (forceful)
- X aids / fuels / boosts / enables / amplifies / accelerates / exacerbates Y.

### Preventative verbs
- X blocks / inhibits / reduces / impedes / prevents / counteracts Y.

### Tentative causal relationships
- X may have contributed to the increase in ...
- X appears to be linked to Y.
- This suggests a weak link may exist between X and Y.
- The findings indicate that X could improve Y.

## 5. Describing Trends and Patterns

### Highlighting trends
- There is a clear trend of increasing / decreasing ...
- Between X and Y there was a significant increase in ...
- The rate of X increased / decreased markedly during ...
- X rose / fell sharply / steadily / gradually between ... and ...

### Describing change
- X underwent a major transformation during ...
- X has changed significantly over the past decade.
- There has been a marked / steady / gradual increase in X since ...

## 6. Signalling Transitions

### Previewing sections
- The following section presents / discusses / describes ...
- In the section that follows, it will be argued that ...
- This chapter is divided into X sections. The first ...

### Introducing a new topic
- Regarding X, / As regards X, / In terms of X, / With respect to X, ...
- Turning now to X, / Moving on to consider X, ...
- Having discussed X, the next section will consider ...

### Linking back
- As was mentioned earlier, ...
- As described in Section X, ...
- Returning to the issue of X, ...

### Shifting viewpoint
- If we now turn to ...
- A related issue is ...
- Another important aspect of X is ...
- A somewhat different aspect of X is discussed in ...

## 7. Comparing and Contrasting

### Comparing similarity
- Similarly, X shows the same pattern as Y.
- In the same way, X and Y both demonstrate ...
- X is comparable to / similar to / analogous to Y.
- Like X, Y also shows ...

### Contrasting
- In contrast, / By contrast, / Conversely, ...
- X differs from Y in several respects.
- Unlike X, Y shows ...
- While X increased, Y decreased.
- Whereas X is driven by ..., Y results from ...

### Concession
- Although / While / Despite / In spite of ...
- X may be true; yet / however / on the other hand ...

## 8. Writing Conclusions

### Summarising findings
- This study has shown that ...
- The present study confirms ...
- The evidence from this study suggests that ...
- This study has identified / found that ...
- The results of this investigation show that ...

### Significance of findings
- The present study makes several contributions to ...
- These findings enhance our understanding of ...
- The current findings add to a growing body of literature on ...

### Limitations
- The study is limited by ...
- A limitation of this study is that ...
- The scope of this study was limited to ...

### Recommendations for future work
- Further research should be undertaken to investigate ...
- Further work is required to establish ...
- A further study with more focus on X is suggested.
- Future studies on the current topic are recommended.
- More research is needed to determine whether ...

## Usage Rules for This Project

1. **Single hedge only**: Never stack hedges ("may possibly", "could potentially"). Pick one.
2. **Causality verbs over vague ones**: Use "drives", "stems from", "gives rise to" instead of "is related to", "is associated with" when the causal direction is established.
3. **Figure references**: Use "Figure X shows" or "As shown in Figure X" patterns. Never "It can be seen that" as filler.
4. **Transitions**: Use topic shifts ("Turning now to", "With respect to") instead of banned openers (Moreover, Furthermore, Additionally).
5. **No Tier 1/2 AI words**: The phrasebank patterns replace the need for AI-flagged vocabulary. If you reach for "pivotal", use "X plays an important role in" instead.
6. **Active conclusions**: "This study has shown" over "It has been shown".
