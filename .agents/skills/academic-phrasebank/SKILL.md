# Academic Phrasebank Skill

A curated collection of sentence templates for academic writing. Use these patterns to maintain scholarly tone, vary sentence structure, and express ideas with precision. Replace bracketed placeholders `[...]` with your specific content.

---

## 1. Introducing a Topic

### Establishing importance
- "[Topic] is a central concern in [field] because [reason]."
- "Understanding [phenomenon] is essential for [practical/theoretical goal]."
- "[Topic] has attracted increasing attention due to [recent development]."
- "The [process/phenomenon] underpins [broader system or application]."

### Defining scope
- "This study focuses on [specific aspect] of [broader topic]."
- "We restrict our analysis to [scope] to isolate [variable/effect]."
- "The present work addresses [narrow question] within the broader context of [field]."

### Identifying the gap
- "Despite extensive research on [topic], [specific aspect] remains poorly understood."
- "Previous studies have largely overlooked [factor/variable]."
- "A key limitation of existing work is [limitation]."
- "No study to date has examined [specific question] using [approach]."
- "While [what is known], the role of [unexplored factor] has received little attention."

### Stating the objective
- "Here, we [verb: quantify/assess/develop/test] [what] using [approach]."
- "The aim of this study is to [objective]."
- "We address this gap by [approach], focusing on [specific aspect]."
- "This paper presents [contribution] and demonstrates [key finding]."

## 2. Describing Methods

### Data and materials
- "We obtained [data type] from [source] covering [spatial/temporal extent]."
- "[Dataset] provides [variable] at [resolution] over the period [time range]."
- "All data were accessed on [date] and are publicly available at [URL/DOI]."
- "We used [N] samples collected from [locations] between [dates]."

### Analytical approach
- "We applied [method] to [quantify/estimate/classify] [target variable]."
- "[Variable] was computed as [formula or description]."
- "To account for [confounding factor], we [correction/adjustment method]."
- "Statistical significance was assessed using [test] at α = [threshold]."
- "We corrected for multiple comparisons using [method, e.g., FDR, Bonferroni]."

### Software and tools
- "All analyses were performed in [language/software] (version [X])."
- "We used the [package name] library (version [X]) for [purpose]."
- "Code to reproduce all analyses is available at [repository URL]."

### Validation
- "We validated our approach by [validation method]."
- "Results were cross-checked against [independent dataset/method]."
- "Sensitivity analyses confirmed that [finding] is robust to [parameter variation]."

## 3. Reporting Findings

### Presenting main results
- "Our analysis reveals that [X% of observations] show [pattern/trend]."
- "[Variable] increased by [amount] over [time period] ([test statistic], p [value])."
- "The [metric] was significantly [higher/lower] in [group A] than [group B] ([statistic])."
- "We found [strong/moderate/weak] evidence for [relationship] between [X] and [Y]."

### Describing spatial or temporal patterns
- "[Pattern] was most pronounced in [region/period]."
- "The effect varied across [spatial units/time periods], ranging from [min] to [max]."
- "[Variable] exhibited a [monotonic/non-linear/seasonal] trend over [time span]."

### Quantifying effects
- "The effect size ([metric] = [value]) corresponds to a [small/medium/large] practical difference."
- "[Treatment] reduced [outcome] by [X]% relative to [control] (95% CI: [lower, upper])."
- "Each unit increase in [predictor] was associated with a [coefficient] change in [response]."

### Null and negative results
- "We found no significant association between [X] and [Y] ([test], p = [value])."
- "Contrary to expectations, [variable] showed no [trend/difference]."
- "The effect of [factor] was negligible ([metric] < [threshold])."

## 4. Comparing Results with Prior Work

### Agreement
- "Consistent with [Author et al. Year], we find that [finding]."
- "Our results corroborate the findings of [Author et al. Year], who reported [finding]."
- "This is in line with [theoretical framework], which predicts [prediction]."

### Disagreement
- "Unlike [Author et al. Year], who found [their finding], our analysis indicates [your finding]."
- "Our results diverge from [Author et al. Year], likely because [reason]."
- "The discrepancy with [prior work] may be attributable to [difference in method/data/scope]."

### Extension
- "Extending the work of [Author et al. Year], we show that [new finding]."
- "While [Author et al. Year] examined [their scope], we broaden the analysis to [your scope]."
- "Our study adds to [Author et al. Year] by incorporating [new variable/method]."

## 5. Hedging and Qualifying

### Appropriate hedging
- "The data suggest that [interpretation]."
- "Our results are consistent with [explanation], although [alternative] cannot be ruled out."
- "This trend may reflect [mechanism], but further investigation is needed."
- "A possible explanation is that [mechanism]."
- "These findings point toward [interpretation], though [caveat]."

### Calibrating confidence
- **Strong**: "The evidence demonstrates..." / "The data clearly show..."
- **Moderate**: "The results indicate..." / "The findings suggest..."
- **Cautious**: "The data are consistent with..." / "It is possible that..."
- **Speculative**: "One might speculate that..." / "A tentative explanation is..."

### Avoid over-hedging
- Bad: "It might possibly be the case that the data could perhaps suggest..."
- Good: "The data suggest..."

### Avoid under-hedging
- Bad: "This proves that X causes Y."
- Good: "This provides strong evidence that X contributes to Y."

## 6. Discussing Causation

### Causal language (when warranted)
- "[Controlled experiment] demonstrated that [X] causes [Y]."
- "Removing [factor] eliminated [effect], confirming a causal role."

### Correlational language (observational studies)
- "[X] was positively associated with [Y] ([r/ρ] = [value], p = [value])."
- "Regions with higher [X] tended to exhibit [Y], suggesting a link between [X] and [Y]."
- "The co-occurrence of [X] and [Y] is consistent with [mechanism], though causality cannot be established from observational data alone."

### Mechanistic explanation
- "A plausible mechanism is that [X] leads to [Y] via [pathway]."
- "This effect may be mediated by [intermediate variable]."
- "The observed pattern is consistent with [physical/biological process]."

---

## 7. Acknowledging Limitations

### Data limitations
- "Our analysis is constrained by [data limitation, e.g., spatial resolution, temporal coverage]."
- "[Dataset] may underestimate [variable] in [regions/conditions] due to [reason]."
- "The limited sample size (N = [X]) restricts the generalizability of these findings."

### Methodological limitations
- "Our approach does not account for [factor], which may influence [outcome]."
- "The assumption of [assumption] may not hold in [specific context]."
- "Alternative methods (e.g., [method]) could yield different results."

### Scope limitations
- "This study focuses on [scope]; findings may not generalize to [broader context]."
- "We did not examine [related factor], which warrants future investigation."
- "Regional differences in [factor] may limit the applicability of these results to [other regions]."

---

## 8. Discussing Future Work

- "Future studies should investigate [specific question]."
- "Extending this analysis to [broader scope/longer period] would clarify [uncertainty]."
- "The integration of [additional data/method] could improve [aspect of analysis]."
- "A promising avenue for future research is [direction]."
- "Experimental validation of [mechanism] would strengthen the evidence for [claim]."
- "Longitudinal studies are needed to determine whether [observed pattern] persists over [longer timescale]."

---

## 9. Transitions and Connectors

### Adding information
- "In addition, [new point]."
- "[Finding] further supports the interpretation that [claim]."
- "A related observation is that [point]."

### Contrasting
- "However, [contrasting point]."
- "By contrast, [alternative finding]."
- "Despite [observation], [contrasting result] was found."
- "Although [concession], [main point]."

### Showing consequence
- "As a result, [consequence]."
- "This implies that [implication]."
- "Consequently, [outcome]."
- "[Finding] therefore suggests that [conclusion]."

### Summarising
- "Taken together, these results indicate that [synthesis]."
- "In summary, [main point]."
- "Overall, the evidence supports [conclusion]."
- "Collectively, these findings suggest that [interpretation]."

## 10. Banned Patterns — Quick Reference

These phrases signal AI-generated text or lazy writing. Avoid them:

| Banned | Use Instead |
|--------|-------------|
| "delve into" | "examine", "investigate" |
| "multifaceted" | "complex", "varied" |
| "Moreover" / "Furthermore" | restructure or delete |
| "plays a crucial role" | "is important for", "contributes to" |
| "paving the way" | "enabling", "opening opportunities for" |
| "sheds light on" | "reveals", "clarifies" |
| "a growing body of evidence" | cite specific studies |
| "in recent years" | give specific dates |
